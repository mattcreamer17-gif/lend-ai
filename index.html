The shape of the problem changed in 2023.

The pivot to brokerage changed what the credit model has to predict. It used to answer one thing, which was whether Lendai should lend. It now has to answer two things: whether any lender will approve, and which partner is the right match. That is a different kind of problem. It is not a larger AI problem.

Most of what runs a cross-border lender is not AI.

It is code, rules, documents, and state-by-state licensing. For Lendai, the ten percent where AI earns its keep is narrow and specific: scoring foreign-national creditworthiness with limited US data, and matching borrowers to the partner whose credit box fits them best. Everything else should be the fastest, cheapest layer that gets the job done.

60% Traditional software and databases
30% Rule-based logic, credit-box overlays, per-state disclosures, routing tables
10% AI: thin-file creditworthiness and partner-match scoring

Governance posture for regulated credit

What a partner lender or a state examiner would want to see.

The paper that underpins how we evaluate AI in regulated credit environments is the Ethics Engine, a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs. Read it at arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742 or the repo at github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine. It is the framework to reach for when an audit asks how the models touching credit decisions are evaluated.

The companion paper on agent context architecture (Interpretable Context Methodology, submitted to ACM TiiS) lives at github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-.
